Chapter V______________Why It Matters

Cities are not experienced equally.

Across the research of the World Health Organization, the OECD, UN-Habitat, the Brookings Institution, and the World Bank, one finding returns again and again: income does not simply describe a person, it shapes the city around them. It determines the air they breathe, the trains they catch, the hospitals they can reach, and the parks their children can play in.

London's own geography confirms this. Four miles separate Westminster from Tower Hamlets — a short train ride, an easy walk — yet the lives inside each borough are shaped by fundamentally different cities. One offers green space as a given; the other treats it as a luxury.

Understanding urban inequality is not a technical exercise. It is the first step in deciding who our cities are for, and what we believe every resident deserves simply by virtue of living there.
